## 1. SpatioTemporal Omics Consortium: a global effort for biological discovery across species, space and time

**Lane:** New or updated.  
**Date:** Published July 9, 2026.  
**Status:** Peer-reviewed Comment in *Nature Methods*.  
**Primary link:** [Nature Methods article](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-026-03150-6)

**Why included:** This is not a modeling method paper, but it is important infrastructure for the next phase of spatial omics modeling. STOC is described as a global, open network to integrate spatial and temporal omics across species, development, disease, and evolution. For modelers, the key value is not one dataset but the coordination layer: shared goals, cross-species scope, spatiotemporal design, and incentives for interoperable resources.

| Resource aspect | Summary |
|---|---|
| Biological scope | Cross-species, developmental, disease, evolutionary, spatial, and temporal omics; positioned as an open international consortium. |
| Modalities | Broad spatial and temporal omics; the Comment emphasizes integration rather than a single assay or modality. |
| Scale | Consortium-scale coordination across participating institutions and funding environments; intended to span species and biological contexts. |
| Access/tooling | The article points to the STOC website and consortium structure rather than a single downloadable dataset. Useful to monitor for standards, resource releases, protocols, and coordinated benchmarks. |
| Modeling uses | Future foundation models, cross-species alignment, spatiotemporal representation learning, perturbation/disease modeling, and benchmark construction will benefit from harmonized spatial-temporal datasets. |
| Reuse caveats | This is a Comment/consortium announcement, not a released atlas with uniform metadata yet. Treat it as a signal to monitor, not as an immediately reusable modeling benchmark. |

## Emerging themes to watch

- **Spatial modeling is expanding into spatiotemporal modeling.** STOC's scope explicitly links spatial measurements with time, development, disease progression, and evolution.
- **Cross-species integration will need stronger representation learning.** Models will need to separate conserved tissue programs from species-specific anatomy, assay effects, and developmental timing.
- **Standards and metadata may become as important as model architecture.** Large coordinated efforts can either enable reusable benchmarks or create incompatible silos, depending on how metadata, ontologies, and access are handled.
- **Do not overinterpret consortium announcements as datasets.** The useful action now is monitoring STOC outputs for concrete atlases, portals, protocols, and benchmark-ready resources.
